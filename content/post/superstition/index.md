Superstition. Is it harmless or dangerous? Should we leave superstitious people alone or should we fight for a world where superstition is a thing of the past and everybody is shocked that anyone ever believed in it?

# What is superstition?

> “Superstition is the belief in supernatural causality—that one event causes another without any natural process linking the two events” – [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superstition)
>  
> “Superstition, belief, half-belief, or practice for which there appears to be no rational substance” – [Encyclopædia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/superstition)
>  
> “belief that is not based on human reason or scientific knowledge, but is connected with old ideas about magic, etc.“ – [Cambridge Dictionary](http://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/superstition)

Superstition is one of those confusing words that have a rather clear definition but that in practice people use it in a quite different way. It has a slightly negative connotation and therefore people rarely identify with the term even if they believe in astrology, homeopathy, crystals, energies etc. In practice, for something to be called superstition, besides the formal definition, it has to be a small, isolated belief that is outside the framework of any big, organized religion, or even a loose set of beliefs like in the case of the new age movement. If you believe black cats bring bad luck, you'll be called superstitious. If you believe Jesus walked over water, you'll be called religious. If you believe in auras, chakras etc, you'll be called new age.
Technically, however, all of these fit the definition of superstition perfectly. Believing in God or immortal souls, a core belief in most religions, is by definition superstition. They are all beliefs “connected with old ideas about magic” and with “no rational substance”.

# What is religion?

> “Religion, human beings’ relation to that which they regard as holy, sacred, absolute, spiritual, divine, or worthy of especial reverence. It is also commonly regarded as consisting of the way people deal with ultimate concerns about their lives and their fate after death.” – [Encyclopædia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion)
>  
> “the belief in and worship of a god or gods, or any such system of belief and worship” – [Cambridge Dictionary](http://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/religion)

Religion is a complex phenomenon that involves many different cultural elements, but one that is rather unanimous in all definitions is superstition.

Most of the time, when I criticize religion, I'm actually criticizing superstition. I'm usually not attacking the cultural aspects of religion. They're too entangled with national identity and other types of non-religious traditions and I believe cultural loyalty is so deeply rooted in human psychology that there's no point in fighting it in such a generic way. Even I, as an atheist, would be upset if I wasn't able to celebrate Christmas for some reason. I've done it all my life, it's part of my culture and it has emotional value for me. The same is certainly true for people of other backgrounds and their rituals.
But Christmas is a harmless ritual. What about more problematic ones? In principle, we could all act like grown ups and reevaluate our traditions every now and then as society progresses, accepting that, if a tradition actually hurts people and has no tangible benefit, it should be adapted to modern times or even abandoned altogether. I think most educated people in sufficiently developed countries, religious and non-religious, would agree that abusive exorcism rituals, witchcraft accusations and animal sacrifices have no place in the 21st century. But why not?

# The hypocrisy of religious ethics

Let's take some time to think about the fundamentals of moral philosophy using the classical [trolley problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trolley_problem). Imagine you're the conductor of a trolley that goes out of control and cannot stop. Ahead of you, there's a group of 5 workers on the tracks who will get killed by the trolley if nothing is done. If you pull the lever, however, you can derail the trolley onto a track with only one worker. Would you pull it? I would. It turns out that up to 90% of us would[ref][Clang Went the Trolley: ‘Would You Kill the Fat Man?’ and ‘The Trolley Problem’ – New York Times](http://www.nytimes.com/2013/11/24/books/review/would-you-kill-the-fat-man-and-the-trolley-problem.html)[/ref]. Now, what if, after you kill the single person, it turns out that the 5 workers on the tracks were actually fake, drawn on a piece of paper that you would have ripped killing nobody if you had refrained from pulling the lever? Does that make you evil?

If you ask Western, secularized Christians what they think of Congonese children who have the devil "beaten out of them"[ref]['They accused me of killing and eating my grandmother': Agony of Congo's 50,000 'child witches' who are brutally exorcised to 'beat the devil out of them' – The Daily Mail](http://www.dailymail.co.uk/news/article-3276057/My-grandmother-died-said-witch-drink-salt-water-stuck-fingers-throat-pieces-said-d-eaten-Congo-s-child-witches-exorcised-devil-beaten-them.html)[/ref], they'll say it's "barbaric". But what if the devil really is inside of them and only a beating can remove it? Wouldn't the beating be justified then? I'll make it simple: yes, it would be perfectly justified. Just like you were justified to pull the lever to kill a single worker instead of the five you thought were going to die instead, even though the belief that motivated your decision was false.
